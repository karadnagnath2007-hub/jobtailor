You spent hours crafting the perfect resume. You have the skills, the experience, and the passion. But you never heard back. The likely culprit? An Applicant Tracking System, or ATS, rejected your resume before a human ever saw it.

## What Is an ATS?

An Applicant Tracking System is software used by employers to collect, sort, scan, and rank the job applications they receive. Think of it as a digital gatekeeper. Companies like Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, and Taleo power the hiring process for most Fortune 500 companies and thousands of smaller businesses.

## How It Reads Your Resume

ATS software parses your resume into a structured database. It looks for:

- **Contact information** in expected locations
- **Work experience** with dates, titles, and companies
- **Education** and certifications
- **Skills** that match the job requisition
- **Keywords** from the job description

When the parser encounters something it doesn't understand — a fancy layout, an unconventional section name, an embedded image — it either skips it or misreads it.

## Common Failure Points

### Creative Formatting
That beautiful two-column resume template? The ATS probably reads it left-to-right across both columns, mixing up your skills with your job descriptions.

### PDF Issues
Not all PDFs are created equal. Some are essentially images. If you "print to PDF" from a design tool, the ATS may see nothing at all.

### Missing Keywords
The job asks for "SEO strategy" and you wrote "improved search rankings." To the ATS, those are completely different things.

### Headers and Footers
Contact info in a header or footer? Many ATS parsers strip those sections entirely.

## The Human Problem

Even when your resume gets through the ATS, it's often presented to the recruiter in a stripped-down, plain-text format. If the parser mangled your carefully designed layout, the recruiter sees a mess.
