Most job seekers don't realize that 80% of resumes never make it past the first screening. Not because the candidate isn't qualified, but because their resume wasn't formatted for the robots that read them first.

## What Actually Happens When You Apply

When you click "submit" on a job application, your resume doesn't go to a hiring manager. It goes to an Applicant Tracking System — software that scans, parses, and ranks your resume against the job description.

These systems look for:
- **Exact keyword matches** from the job description
- **Standard section headings** (Experience, Education, Skills)
- **Clean formatting** without tables, columns, or graphics
- **Relevant job titles** and skills in the right places

## The Keyword Gap Problem

Here's the most common reason resumes fail: the job description says "project management" but your resume says "led initiatives." The ATS doesn't know those mean the same thing. It counts exact matches.

**Solution**: Mirror the language in the job description. If they ask for "cross-functional collaboration," use that exact phrase, not "worked with different teams."

## Formatting Rules That Matter

1. **Use a single column** — ATS parsers struggle with multi-column layouts
2. **Stick to standard fonts** — Arial, Calibri, Georgia
3. **Avoid headers/footers** — important info gets stripped
4. **Save as .docx or plain-text-friendly PDF** — not all PDFs parse equally
5. **No images, charts, or icons** — they're invisible to ATS
